# The Art of Being Still

## Meditation is not sitting, and it is not walking

We tend to think of meditation as a posture — legs folded, back straight, eyes closed, holding ourselves rigid in a corner. But meditation is none of these things. It is not sitting and it is not walking. It is setting aside everything else and giving attention only to what is being felt in this present moment. That, and nothing more elaborate than that, is meditation.

There is a teaching the Buddha gave — _ekāyana magga_, the one and only path for the purification of beings — and from the Four Foundations of Mindfulness we take a method and try to enter, through it, the path that leads toward liberation. But stripped to its simplest form, all of this means one thing: cultivating awareness. Living with awareness. Knowing that you are here, now, in this moment.

## You are always already here

You are reading this in the present moment. You were not living in the past, and you are not stepping into the future. You are always, every single time, right here in this moment.

Test it for yourself. Try to recall what you did when you woke this morning — getting out of bed, reaching for your phone, walking somewhere. Notice that the memory is arising _now_, inside a present thought. Your past exists only as a thought happening in this moment. There is no problem with that — you are here in the present while remembering it.

Now plan tomorrow. What will you cook tonight; will you eat at all? Notice again: that planning, too, arises here, in this present moment. Your future lives in this moment as well. The mind runs to the past, runs to the future, drags up one thing after another — but the only place you have ever actually stood is here.

So do not be troubled when the mind runs. Even while it travels through past and future, the time it spends doing so is the present moment. Mindfulness simply means catching this: realising, while you are mourning some old thing, that the past is a thought in the present; realising, while you are planning, that the planning happens now; realising, when a sensation arises in the body, that it too is occurring in this moment. That recognition is all that mindfulness is.

## "I have stopped"

When the bandit Aṅgulimāla chased the Buddha, the Buddha said: _I have stopped — you stop._ That is what we are trying to do.

The Buddha said "I have stopped" while still walking. So stopping does not mean halting the body. It means no longer running along _with_ the mind. Aṅgulimāla was running with his mind, carried along by it. To stop is to step out of that running. And only once you have stopped can you actually _see_ the running mind.

Stopping is not clenching the legs and gritting the teeth and sitting stiffly. When you do walking meditation, stopping means watching how the walking happens. Ordinarily we walk while our minds are elsewhere — admiring the surroundings, thinking of a hundred other things — and the legs simply carry on automatically. The breath rises and falls automatically. The eyelids blink automatically. We are somewhere else entirely.

Who is it, then, that brushes your teeth in the morning? Who lifts the hand? Who eats the meal while your attention is on the television? You sit watching a program and the rice goes to the mouth, the curry is gathered up, a piece of fish is found and eaten and the hand returns — and you were not there for any of it. Who was eating? Who was feeding you?

## The cloth that will not come clean

There is an old story of a monk, Cūḷapanthaka, who was given a small white cloth and told to rub it while repeating a phrase. As he rubbed, the white cloth grew soiled, and watching that change, he came to realisation.

Our mothers rub cloth their whole lives. They scrub and scrub with soap, year after year. The monk rubbed cloth once and woke up. You scrub a soiled cloth and it comes clean. Where is the difference? Somewhere there is a gap, and the gap is this: mindfulness. He had it; the ordinary scrubbing does not.

Notice what happens when you are washing without mindfulness. The work feels like a burden. You ask the children to pile up the laundry; you complain; you wish someone else would do it. But if you were present — if awareness were a kind of investigation, an exploration of what is actually happening — something shifts. Lift a cup and set it down, lift it and set it down, and attend closely: the firmness in the hand, the weight, the contact. There is even a quiet pleasure in simply doing the thing. With mindfulness, washing cloth can be done gladly. Without it, the same act is misery. The two acts are identical; only awareness differs.

## A toy you can finally set down

What is being offered here is, in a sense, a toy — and the offer is that the long game the mind has been playing with you can now end.

Until now the mind has set the tune and you have danced; everywhere you go, you play out its little games. The mind tells you to do a thing, and once you do it, the same mind turns and asks why you did it. It manufactures a feeling, presents it as real, and you obey. It is, as the talk puts it, an exhausting harassment — and the way out is not to argue with it but to take up mindfulness and _watch_ it. Watch carefully what it is actually doing. Then the dead-ends it built, the detours, the cunning illusory turns, all begin to show themselves. And you find yourself laughing at the trick of it.

This continues into sleep. We do not know _how_ we fall asleep — it happens automatically. But with mindfulness held steady, you can begin to see even the way sleep arrives. The mind subdues us, puts us under, and shows us dreams; and in the dream too we are frightened, harassed, made to act and then reproached for acting. Enough of being managed like that. Take up awareness and watch what the mind is doing.

## Watching the body

The method begins with the body. In the contemplation of the body — _kāyānupassanā_ — you simply feel your own body: its weight pressing into the ground, the whole field of bodily sensation. You hold attention there.

It is said that liberation is to be realised within this fathom-long body itself; the whole world, in a sense, is found within it. So this is the method — to dwell together with your own body. While the mind is on the body, the breath becomes apparent. You do not need to breathe deliberately; the breath is happening anyway. Just watch it: the belly moving out and back, the air at the nostrils, sometimes cool, sometimes warm. Watch whether it enters more through one nostril than the other. Watch the sweat, the warmth, the cold, an itch, an ache in the leg — every sensation, recognised as occurring _now_.

When you sit in formal meditation, keep the spine upright — not by straining the back, which only creates new pain in the neck and shoulders, but by placing attention at the very base of the spine. Rest awareness there and the body draws itself upright on its own, like a released spring. The Buddha's own instruction was this: hold the body straight, and set mindfulness before you. That is all that "sitting meditation" means.

## Meeting pain

Sensations will come. A pain arises in the leg, and the mind immediately announces, _your leg hurts — change position._ And usually we obey at once.

But understand first: the mind does not want to be observed, and from the moment you begin to meditate it comes at you like a war — restless craving stirring, agitating, building. It fabricates the pain, and then on the strength of that fabrication it orders you to move.

So before you shift, do this. The mind says the leg hurts — ask it _where_. The whole leg does not hurt; some small place does. Narrow it down. _Where, exactly?_ Keep asking, keep narrowing, until the pain is no longer a vague region but a tiny point — a dot. And a dot is not a catastrophe; it is just a small problem. Hold your attention precisely on that dot, focus on it like a fine needle going straight to the nerve. And here is the strange and wonderful thing: as you do, the pain leaves. As long as we refuse to look at it, the mind keeps it real for us. But narrowed, refined, examined closely — _what is actually here?_ — you find for yourself that there is nothing there after all.

There is an image for this. People go to shrines where a curtain hangs, hiding what is behind it; they light their lamps and make their offerings and dance, all of them caught up in it — except one person, who slips behind the curtain and looks. And behind it there is nothing. The crowd is deceived only because no one draws the curtain. So learn this skill: when something arises to fool you, go right up to it, draw the curtain, and look. _What is actually here?_

## Your path is your own

When you walk in meditation, keep some small points in mind. Which part of the sole touches the ground first; which part lifts last. As one foot rises, the whole weight falls on the other — for that instant you are standing on a single leg. Notice how the feet are actually placed. We imagine we walk straight and even, but with real attention you see the truth: one foot turned out, one foot askew, an odd little gait that mindfulness alone reveals. You will laugh at yourself, seeing through the illusion of how you thought you moved.

Look at worn footwear and you can read it — one person's soles wear at the toe, another's at the heel — because each of us presses down differently. Each person has their own signature, their own particular marks. This is why you cannot simply borrow someone else's method, hear what another person did and assume it is the way for you. The fingerprint each of us carries cannot be copied onto anyone else, and meditation is the same. So when you come to practice, learn _your_ marks. Ask what is happening for you, in this present moment.

## Set the knowledge aside

One warning runs through all of this: do not apply your accumulated knowledge to the present moment.

Set all of it aside — what you learned in school, what you know about the Dhamma, what you know about the world, the problems at work, the troubles at home, the economy, politics, every datum you have ever gathered. Set it down. And then give attention only to what is being felt in this present moment. That is meditation. That is the cultivation of mindfulness.

You do not need to recite anything, or summon any particular contemplation, or hold the mind in some special technique. Those are separate methods for separate times. Here, maintaining mindfulness simply means recognising what is happening now. Keep awareness on the body, and the breath appears. Keep it there through every posture — sitting, standing, walking, lying down — and through every ordinary act. When the mind runs, do not be upset; in that moment you have at least _known_ that it ran, and you can return, gently, to the breath or to whatever is being felt.

It is, in the end, astonishingly simple. There is no grand story to it. You only have to wake to the present moment, to stay with it in awareness, to know what is happening right now. You are always already here. The whole of the practice is to notice it.
