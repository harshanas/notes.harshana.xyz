The Vampire Generation: Scott Galloway on Masculinity, Money, and the Crisis of the Young Man

May 1, 2026

A structured essay drawn from Scott Galloway’s conversation with Andrew Huberman on the Huberman Lab podcast.

Introduction#

Across a wide-ranging conversation with neuroscientist Andrew Huberman, NYU professor Scott Galloway lays out a sweeping diagnosis of what is going wrong for young men in America today — and a set of unusually concrete prescriptions for what they, and the rest of society, can do about it. Galloway is a self-described progressive, a serial entrepreneur, and an educator who has built much of his public work around the economic and emotional plight of young men. The conversation moves through masculinity, big tech, alcohol, pornography, the dating market, and intergenerational wealth transfer, but a single thread runs through all of it: the systems young men are coming of age inside have been quietly redesigned to extract from them, and most of the adults in the room are either profiting from that arrangement or looking away.

This essay organizes Galloway’s argument into its main pillars.

I. A Code for Living: Provider, Protector, Procreator#

Galloway begins with a deceptively simple claim: every person needs a code. Life is a stream of small decisions, and what separates people who do well from people who don’t is making a higher proportion of good decisions than the peer group around them. Codes — whether drawn from religion, the military, family, sport, or work — are what allow you to make those decisions consistently rather than reinventing yourself every morning.

For young men in particular, he argues for a code built around three aspirational masculine traits. The first is provider: have a plan to be economically relevant. This doesn’t require Goldman Sachs or Harvard; it can mean trade school and an HVAC license. What matters is that a young man assumes he will, at some point, need to carry economic weight for a family, and orients early toward that possibility. The second is protector: develop strength, skills, and presence such that the people around you feel safe. Galloway describes the deepest masculine satisfaction he knows as the moment at night when his family is asleep and protected because of work he has done. The third is procreator: stop demonizing young men’s desire for relationships and sex. Channeled correctly, that desire is fuel — it makes a young man want to shower, work out, build a kindness practice, demonstrate excellence, and endure rejection. Like fire in a steel casing, contained sexual motivation produces progress.

Galloway acknowledges this framework is incomplete. The deeper measure, lent to him by the scholar Richard Reeves, is surplus value: do you, in the honest accounting, give more than you take? More tax revenue than you absorb, more love than you receive, more listening than complaining? Many men, he says, are born male but die without ever becoming men in this sense. Becoming a man is not a ceremony or an age — it is the moment you can look in the mirror and say you produce more than you consume.

II. The Tactical Program: Three Things, Done Weekly#

Where the code is philosophical, Galloway’s actual mentoring program is brutally concrete. With young men he works with — most of them struggling, many still living at home — he asks to see their phone. Within minutes he can usually find eight hours a day being spent on TikTok, X, YouTube, gambling, and porn. That time is the asset they have, and the work is to reallocate it into three buckets:

  1. Get strong. Move weights, build bulk, or run far. The male body has a window in which it is uniquely capable, and wasting it produces the worst antidepressant deficit Galloway can think of. Three workouts a week, minimum.

  2. Make money outside the house. Not necessarily a career — Lyft driving, TaskRabbit, or Panera at $18 an hour all qualify. Once a young man tastes earned money, capitalism starts to make sense to him. The point is reps, not income.

  3. Show up in groups, in the service of something. A nonprofit, a church group, a sports league, a writing club — three times a month, at minimum. Within that, practice “the approach”: ask someone to do something. Ask a guy to watch a Jets game with you. Ask a woman to grab a coffee. The goal, Galloway insists, is not yes — it is the no. Everyone admirable got where they are by absorbing an enormous volume of nos. Big tech’s algorithms now promise a frictionless life, and a frictionless life produces people with no capacity to endure rejection.

A young man who works out three times a week, works thirty hours a week outside the house, and volunteers, Galloway argues, is immediately in the top decile of young men. The mating market and the labor market both reward him from there.

III. Big Tech as Antagonist#

The villain in Galloway’s account is unambiguous: big tech. Forty percent of the S&P 500 is now ten companies whose central business is monetizing human attention. Their AI systems run a million experiments a second to find ways to extract one more second of engagement. The result, Galloway argues, is a generation of “asocial, asexual males” who reach thirty having never built the skills required for adult professional or romantic life — obese, anxious, depressed, sequestered from their peers, and convinced this is normal.

He is careful to distinguish a net judgment from a critical one. He would not press a button to delete big tech or Elon Musk. EVs, SpaceX, the productivity gains of the last two decades — these are real goods. But “net good” is not a defense against accountability. He argues the wealthiest man in the world is in many ways an extraordinary masculine role model — focused, risk-tolerant, capable of marshalling capital around vision — and simultaneously a poor one, in that he punches down at people with vastly less power and shows little appreciation for the American system that made his fortune possible.

Huberman pushes back, suggesting we should treat public figures as a buffet — adopt the traits worth adopting, leave the rest. Galloway concedes the point, but holds the line on scrutiny: the more power, attention, and capital a person has accumulated, the more legitimate it is to examine what they do with it.

The structural problem, Galloway argues, is that we have built an economy in which antagonism is the most valuable commodity online. A nuanced, generous comment doesn’t trip the algorithm; an inflammatory one does. The result is a society in which Americans now perceive other Americans as their primary enemy, a dynamic actively amplified by foreign actors who have correctly identified that they cannot beat the United States economically or militarily but can absolutely get it to hate itself.

IV. Solutions for Big Tech#

Galloway’s proposed remedies are not radical:

  • Antitrust. Meta should never have been allowed to acquire Instagram; Google should never have been allowed to acquire YouTube. Competition would create oxygen for platforms with different content standards.
  • Remove Section 230 protections for algorithmically elevated content. A platform that chooses what to amplify is a media company and should bear the same liability.
  • Age-gate the products. The downside of Instagram and TikTok for a fourteen-year-old, he argues, dwarfs the upside.

None of this happens, in his telling, because lobbying produces the highest ROI in the American economy. Amazon alone employs more full-time lobbyists in Washington than there are sitting U.S. senators. Until that changes, common-sense regulation will continue to die quietly in committee.

V. The Mating Market and the Alliance Between Men and Women#

Some of the conversation’s most striking material concerns dating. Galloway describes a sex recession produced jointly by dating apps with an economic interest in keeping users searching, and a public discourse that has taught the genders to distrust one another. He pushes back hard on two myths in particular. The first is the myth that a man making a respectful approach risks his career — he calls this an excuse for inceldom. The second is the symmetric myth that women face serious physical danger on dates with strangers — statistically, he notes, the Uber ride is more dangerous than the date, and a man on a date is roughly sixteen times more likely to harm himself afterward than to harm her.

The alliance he most wants to see renewed is the one between men and women. He calls it the greatest alliance in history, and argues that women’s economic ascent over the last fifty years is precisely why America did not become a second-tier power to China. Men’s descent is not caused by women’s rise; treating it as if it were is one of the most damaging stories online culture currently tells.

Huberman raises, almost reluctantly, a tension he encounters constantly: a real woman in his life had asked him to thank Galloway for his work, then described a generation of men in San Francisco who avoid empathy, repair, romance, and protective behavior because each of those things demands accountability and growth. Galloway hears the description and concedes it is a tall building to scale — but argues that the prescription is the same. Get fit, get earning, get into rooms with people, and start making approaches. Most of what young men fear about the dating market is fictional. Most of what they actually need is reps.

VI. Pornography and the Substitution of Simulation for Life#

Galloway names pornography as possibly the most under-researched addiction of the present moment. The reason, he suspects, is that no serious academic wants to be known as the porn professor. But the consequences are visible. He uses his own UCLA years as an example: one of his motivators for getting onto campus was the non-zero possibility of meeting a woman. Strip out that motivation by giving him lifelike pornography on a screen at home, he says, and he is not sure he would have built any of the social skills that turned out to matter.

Huberman extends the frame. Phone use, he argues, is less an addiction in the dopamine-hit sense and closer to clinical obsessive-compulsive disorder: a compulsion that does not relieve the obsession but reinforces it. The most dopaminergic circuits in the brain are tied not just to sex but to anger and frustration — circuits that, unlike eating or sex, never satiate. This, he suggests, is the real circuit big tech has tapped: not pleasure, but the inexhaustible arousal of grievance.

VII. Alcohol, Cannabis, and Social Lubrication#

The two men disagree, civilly, about alcohol. Huberman’s well-known position is that zero is healthier than any, and that two drinks a week is probably the ceiling for most people optimizing for vitality. Galloway, while deferring to the science, makes a social argument: in an era of remote work, closed pubs, and atomized young people, the marginal risk to a healthy 25-year-old’s liver is dwarfed by the risk of social isolation. He floats lowering the U.S. drinking age back to 18, partly on the British model — letting young people ease into alcohol in supervised, social settings rather than discovering it in college through binge drinking. He concedes the obvious counterpoint: in an age of phones, drunken stupidity is recorded forever.

On cannabis he is more personal. He smoked heavily in college, stopped for two decades, and now uses edibles roughly twice a week as a sleep aid. He acknowledges Huberman’s caveats — that young men with a genetic predisposition to psychosis or bipolar disorder need to be especially careful, and that for an unhealthy, isolated 25-year-old, neither alcohol nor cannabis is the right answer. The right answer is the gym.

VIII. The Vampire Generation#

The essay’s hardest section, in Galloway’s telling, is the intergenerational one. He calls his own cohort — the late boomers and early Gen X — the vampire generation: never drafted, never asked to serve, the beneficiaries of unprecedented prosperity at historically low tax rates, now voting themselves more and more of the budget.

The numbers he cites are arresting. The average 70-year-old is 72% wealthier than the 70-year-old of forty years ago; the average 25-year-old is 24% poorer. Forty percent of federal spending now goes to Americans over 65, on track for fifty percent within a decade. Social Security is funded by a payroll tax that caps at $160,000 — meaning a young employee earning $150,000 pays the same dollar amount as a multi-millionaire. The two largest tax deductions in the code are mortgage interest and capital gains: who owns homes and stocks? Older Americans. Who rents and earns wages? Younger ones. The transfer is not metaphorical. It is the explicit architecture of the tax code.

Galloway extends the same argument to medicine and science funding. Vast resources go to extending the lives of people over 65; almost none to studying the addictions, despair, and social isolation killing people under 40. He suggests, half-seriously, the Japanese model of mandatory academic retirement at 65, freeing grant money and lab space for the next generation. The point is not that older people are worthless — many are reservoirs of wisdom — but that a society which loves the unremarkable young person, the one who isn’t a freakishly accomplished 18-year-old, is the kind of society that creates room for someone like him to fail his way to UCLA on appeal and eventually give twenty million dollars back.

That America, he argues, has been replaced by one that worships the freakishly remarkable and the children of the rich, and tells everyone else they are out of luck.

IX. Mandatory National Service and Other Common-Sense Repairs#

If Galloway could pass one policy, it would be mandatory national service. Not necessarily military — senior care, conservation, animal shelters, smoke-jumping. The two countries with the lowest rates of young-adult depression in the West, he notes, are Israel and Singapore, both of which require national service. Service produces purpose, mixing of classes and races, exposure to danger and responsibility, and the experience of being part of something larger than oneself.

Beyond that, he proposes:

  • Strip universities of their tax-exempt status if they have endowments over a billion dollars and are not growing their freshman classes faster than the population.
  • Require 20% of degrees to be in non-traditional, vocational, or specialty programs as a condition of tax-free status.
  • Tax private K–12 schools and reinvest in public ones.
  • Decouple property taxes from school funding, so a kid’s zip code doesn’t determine the quality of their education.
  • Reform Citizens United and end gerrymandering so elections stop being decided by whoever raises the most money.

X. The Last Mile: Male Mentorship#

The conversation closes on the most personal of his prescriptions. The single best predictor of a boy coming off the rails, Galloway says, is the loss of a male role model — through death, divorce, or abandonment. At that moment, a boy becomes more likely to be incarcerated than to graduate from college. And men, even successful men, are not stepping up. There are roughly three times as many women applying to be Big Sisters in New York as men applying to be Big Brothers.

Some of this, he acknowledges, is the long shadow of clergy abuse and other scandals — men have become wary of being seen as predatory simply for showing interest in a boy who isn’t theirs. But the cost of that wariness has been catastrophic. The need is enormous, and the bar for adding value is low. He describes mentees asking him whether they should move to Alaska on a whim, or whether a diet of pineapple juice and creatine is a good idea. You do not need a degree in adolescent psychiatry to answer those questions. You need to be a man living a roughly virtuous life, willing to ask, “Did you get outside today? Are you working out?”

The ultimate expression of masculinity, in Galloway’s framework, is not self-care or even care for one’s own family. It is getting involved in the life of a child who isn’t yours. Find a single mother at work. Offer to take her son to a game. Wash a car together. Show up. The country has more single-parent households than any other nation on earth, and the easiest fix that doesn’t involve government is simply more men deciding to be men in the lives of boys who need one.

Conclusion#

Galloway’s argument hangs together because it operates on every level at once. The personal: have a code, get strong, earn money, endure rejection. The relational: stop blaming the other gender, renew the alliance, mentor someone. The civic: tax the wealth transfer, regulate big tech, restore service. He is unsparing about his own generation’s selfishness and equally unsparing about the young men who use that selfishness as an excuse not to level up.

The conversation ends with Huberman thanking him for defying the stereotypes of the political tribes that try to claim him. That defiance is, in a sense, the whole point. Galloway’s diagnosis cannot be metabolized by either the manosphere or the wing of the left that treats young men as a problem to be managed. It asks something harder of everyone — that men be more useful, that women’s gains be celebrated and not resented, that the old pay forward instead of voting themselves more, and that the young accept that nothing about a good life has ever been frictionless. The data, Galloway insists, is on his side. The willingness to act on it is the question.
